Subjective Hydrography Maps Created by the Children of Pisacane School in Torpignattara Associated with the Casilino Ad Duas Lauros Ecomuseum

(March-May 2022) Link to the project – accessible only via Wayback Machine

During the educational workshops at Pisacane Elementary School, my colleagues and I introduced the theme of water from a geographical perspective, focusing on the hydrological aspects of the Torpignattara neighborhood (East Rome).

We provided the school classes with blank maps and supervised their geography lessons, assisting the children in populating the maps. Each group was given the freedom to enrich the maps with points of interest of their choice, as long as they were water-related.

Demo Produced for School Tours of the I. Kant High School in Rome (November-December 2021)

A web map created in preparation for the guided tours organized by the students of I. Kant High School in Rome.

After a series of theoretical and hands-on lessons on geography, historical outreach, and the history of the Casilino neighborhood in Rome, I developed this map as a guide for the student groups leading the tours. The aim was to provide them with suggestions on which stops to include in their tours, what multimedia sources to prepare, and how to present their findings effectively.

Georeferencing and Study of the Agro Romano Series Maps from the Pio-Gregorian Cadastre

Return to projects

Produced for the thesis of the 2nd-level Master's Program in Smart Governance and GIS Tools at Roma Tre University (2021)

During my internship at U-Space srl, I obtained high-definition copies of certain maps from the Pio-Alexandrine Roman Cadastre related to the eastern quadrant of Rome. The goal of my research was to analyze land use in the examined maps (sheets 24, 32, 42, and 50). To achieve this, I georeferenced the historical maps provided by the State Archives of Rome to U-Space and migrated the information from the cadastral registers (associated with the maps) first into data tables and then into geodata.

Consulting the registers was the most challenging part of the research due to the difficult pandemic situation, which restricted free access to the archives and limited the number of scholars allowed entry. Since each register (like each map) had been created by different groups of surveyors and experts, the consultation required careful supervision of the sources rather than an indiscriminate use of OCR technology.

I presented my research during my thesis defense, projecting a web map that summarized the results of my work to the graduation committee. From this raw map, one could immediately perceive the differences between the cadastral parcels closer to the city walls and thus to the center of Rome—with smaller plots, higher population density, and a near absence of land designated for grazing—along with a denser network of roads and paths. It even highlighted proto-industrial structures tied to local waterways, such as fulling mills (called varche in the Roman dialect).

The comparison with the current image of the now nearly fully urbanized neighborhoods of Rome was made possible by the preservation of certain archaeological structures (such as the walls and Roman aqueducts) as well as more recent structures like manor houses and towers, which today define the toponymy of some areas in Rome.
